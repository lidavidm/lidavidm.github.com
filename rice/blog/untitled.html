Untitled

By Unknown Author
Sun, 21 Sep 2014
  • Tags:
  • India

“It’s good for you,” said my mother as she saw me grimace when she placed another piece of ginger in my mouth. Sitting in the backseat of the car, clutching the door handle, I felt my head spin. The feeling was all too familiar. Only after five hours did the travel sickness subside after we arrived in the district of Nasik, India. My mother often traveled to various districts for work and whenever possible, brought me along with her. In this visit, she was to oversee various social welfare schemes in the region, one of which included the introduction of smokeless chulhe (rustic stoves) in rural households. A short film on new interventions illustrated that by eliminating smoke, the new stoves would reduce the immediate risk of respiratory ailments and the long term effects of air pollution. Although I was impressed by the idea, my mother learned that the women, the supposed beneficiaries, did not share the same sentiment, preferring their former stoves. This was because the heat which escaped from the earlier stoves helped dry the washing which they hung indoors and the smoke released kept insects and mosquitoes at bay. At the time, their reasoning made sense to me as well. Why bring change when it isn’t wanted?

With a little insight into the needs and the culture of a community, people can surely be engaged in the development process.

Years later, I took to studying the rural Indian economy in greater depth. I read a success story in which similar ergonomic stoves were made by the beneficiaries themselves with local materials. As active participants in the program, they accepted the change quite easily. Thinking back to the women in Nasik, I understood that the smokeless chulhe were certainly “for their own good”. However, just as the pungent taste of ginger repelled me from the remedy, losing the perceived advantages of the primitive stoves discouraged the villagers from adopting change. Considerable rigor had gone into developing and supplying the improved stoves. However, this technical innovation could bring change only when people participated in it and accepted it. With a little insight into the needs and the culture of a community, people can surely be engaged in the development process. It was when I understood the benefits of ginger and acquired a taste for it that I began to enjoy the ride.
